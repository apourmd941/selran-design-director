# Voice: How Each Direction Writes

The skill enforces a visual register for each of the seven directions. Without a matching written register, the output hits the uncanny valley: a Brutalist zine that reads like a SaaS landing page, an Editorial essay that says "unlock your potential," a Technical-Minimal product page stuffed with "empower" and "seamless." Visual + copy misalignment is more obvious than either failure alone — it is the single clearest AI tell after purple-pink gradients. Treat copy as a typographic material: it has to match the weight, pace, and temperature of the design.

Consult this file before writing any headline, body paragraph, button label, error message, tooltip, or caption. Match the profile to whichever direction `design-system.md` (or the active starter) is pinned to, then run the cross-direction forbidden-word filter as a last pass.

---

## 1. Editorial

**One-line:** measured, reported, essayistic — a writer with a byline, not a marketer with a deck.

**Sentence length:** average 18–26 words; max ~40. Long sentences are allowed when they earn it through clause rhythm. One short sentence per paragraph as punctuation.

**Verb energy:** descriptive and observational, not imperative. Third person comfortable. Active voice, but the subject is often an idea ("The close is not the problem; the month is"), not a user being told what to do.

**Vocabulary:** argues, notes, reports, suggests, observes, contends, holds, finds. Reach for a slightly elevated register — "a habit hardened," "the case for." Avoid: unlock, empower, leverage, game-changer, revolutionize, seamless, powerful, robust, cutting-edge, best-in-class.

**Punctuation:** em-dashes for asides, semicolons for clause-joining, commas used like an essayist uses them. Italics for emphasis (never bold). No exclamation marks. No "!" in CTAs. Quotation marks are curly.

**Headline pattern:**
- "A quieter case for finance done well."
- "The spreadsheet, reconsidered."
- "After the quarter: notes on pace, posture, and pricing."

**Body pattern:**
> Somewhere between the spreadsheet and the quarterly review, a habit hardened: treat the ledger as a chore, the close as a crisis, and the numbers as something to be reshaped after the fact. {Brand} argues for the opposite — that the books should be correct as they happen.

> Three operators, three different stages, one shared complaint: they stopped waiting for month-end to know where they stood. What they learned is worth reading twice.

**CTA language:** "Read the issue," "Continue reading," "Subscribe," "Open an account," "Request an introduction." Never "Get started now!" or "Unlock access."

**Never says:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Supercharge your workflow"
- "Join thousands of happy customers"
- "Game-changing"
- Any sentence that ends in an exclamation mark

## 2. Technical-Minimal

**One-line:** declarative, short, evidence-led — the doc a senior engineer would sign off on.

**Sentence length:** average 10–16 words; max ~24. Fragments acceptable when they carry a fact. Cut adjectives before you cut nouns.

**Verb energy:** active and precise. Imperative in CTAs and docs ("Open account," "See pricing"). Present tense. The subject is usually the product or the user.

**Vocabulary:** posts, reconciles, imports, exports, queries, returns, sync, fails, retries, runs. Numbers, units, file types, protocol names. Avoid: elegant, seamless, powerful, beautiful, magical, effortless, delightful, smart (as a puff adjective), next-generation.

**Punctuation:** periods. Semicolons occasionally, when joining two short clauses of equal weight. Em-dashes sparingly. Parentheses for units and qualifiers: "(up to $5M)", "(4h SLA)". No exclamation marks anywhere.

**Headline pattern:**
- "Every dollar posted, categorized, and reconciled the moment it moves."
- "Priced by transaction volume, not seats."
- "Type-safe by default. Fast by design."

**Body pattern:**
> No import jobs. No reconciliation queue. No month-end archaeology. The ledger is the product — not a report you run once a month.

> Up to 5,000 transactions per month. Driver-based forecasting. Treasury sweep up to $5M. Audit-ready trail and exports. Priority support, 4h SLA.

**CTA language:** "Open account," "See pricing," "Read the docs," "Talk to us," "Start free," "View on GitHub." Two to four words. Never "Unlock your potential," "Discover more," or anything with "!".

**Never says:**
- "Seamlessly integrates"
- "Empowers teams to..."
- "Supercharge"
- "Revolutionary"
- "World-class"

## 3. Bold-Distinctive

**One-line:** confident, declarative, a little theatrical — manifesto voice, not ad voice.

**Sentence length:** average 6–14 words. Max ~20. Heavy use of one- and two-word hero lines ("Not for everyone." / "Loud on purpose.").

**Verb energy:** imperative and declarative. Short active verbs. The headline is often a claim, not a description.

**Vocabulary:** make, build, break, ship, refuse, insist, earn, own. Reach for sturdy Anglo-Saxon verbs; avoid Latinate puff ("utilize," "facilitate," "leverage"). No "solutions."

**Punctuation:** hard periods. Em-dashes for punch. Colons to set up a claim. Exclamation marks only if they're part of the design statement (and rare even then). Often no period on a display line — typography carries the weight.

**Headline pattern:**
- "Built for the 3%."
- "Loud on purpose. Quiet where it counts."
- "We made a tool. You make the work."

**Body pattern:**
> Every feature we shipped this year came from someone on the team using the product for their own work. That is not a strategy. That is a condition of employment.

> This is not the fastest tool. Not the cheapest. Not the biggest. It is the one that does the specific thing we care about, better than anything else. If that's not you, we understand.

**CTA language:** "Start building," "See the manifesto," "Join the list," "Buy it," "Apply." Often verb-first, no object needed.

**Never says:**
- "One-stop shop"
- "Solutions for modern teams"
- "World's leading"
- Anything that could appear in a Gartner quadrant
- "Trusted by Fortune 500 companies"

## 4. Dark-Premium

**One-line:** cinematic, confident, restrained — a product launch written by someone who respects the reader's time.

**Sentence length:** average 12–20 words; max ~28. Mixes a long setup sentence with a short payoff line.

**Verb energy:** active, low-key, often implicit. Not imperative-heavy — descriptive with a felt subject. Third person about the product, second person rare and considered.

**Vocabulary:** engineered, measured, calibrated, crafted (used once, never twice), tuned, built. Numbers and specifications are heroic here — cite them. Avoid: luxurious, premium, elite, exclusive (all tell, never show), world-class, unparalleled.

**Punctuation:** em-dashes for asides, periods for weight. Colons to introduce a spec. Numbers written as numerals ("0.3 seconds," "17 sensors"). Small-caps metadata in mono.

**Headline pattern:**
- "Seventeen sensors. One result."
- "A watch designed to be read in the dark."
- "Performance, measured where it counts."

**Body pattern:**
> The movement is assembled by hand in Biel, Switzerland, by a team of six. Every piece leaves with a serial number, a card, and the name of the person who finished it.

> We tested it at 200 meters. At 300. At 400. The result below is what came back.

**CTA language:** "Reserve yours," "See specifications," "Book a viewing," "Request access," "Read the white paper." Considered, slightly formal. No "Buy now!!"

**Never says:**
- "Luxurious experience"
- "Premium feel"
- "The ultimate ___"
- "Exclusive access" (show it, don't say it)
- "Unparalleled"

## 5. Warm-Approachable

**One-line:** plainspoken, warm, human — a letter from a small business owner, not a brand team.

**Sentence length:** average 12–18 words; max ~24. Conversational rhythm; occasional short sentences for warmth, not punch.

**Verb energy:** active but gentle. Uses "we" naturally. Second person is friendly, not instructive. Contractions welcome (we're, you'll, don't). Imperatives are invitations ("Come say hi").

**Vocabulary:** make, bake, pour, welcome, gather, share, small, local, by hand, in person. Reach for concrete nouns over abstract ones — "a loaf," not "product." Avoid: cutting-edge, revolutionary, disruptive, best-in-class, any tech-bro jargon, "game-changer," "ecosystem," "synergy."

**Punctuation:** periods and commas, warmly. Em-dashes okay. Exclamation marks allowed, sparingly — one per page, saved for something that genuinely deserves it. Parentheses for friendly asides.

**Headline pattern:**
- "Bread that was flour this morning."
- "We're a family of four. This is what we do."
- "Come in. The coffee's on."

**Body pattern:**
> We open at 7. The croissants are done by 7:30 on a good day, 8 on a slow one. If you come by at 11 and the case is empty, that means today was a good day and we're sorry — we'll see you tomorrow.

> Everything on the menu is made by three people in a kitchen the size of a parking space. If it takes a minute longer than you'd like, that's probably why.

**CTA language:** "Come say hi," "See the menu," "Reserve a table," "Join the list," "Find us." Warm, direct, human.

**Never says:**
- "Elevate your experience"
- "Disrupting the ___ industry"
- "Best-in-class"
- "Solutions"
- "Synergy," "ecosystem," "stakeholder"

## 6. Brutalist / Raw

**One-line:** blunt, factual, system-font honest — the HTML-first voice of someone who refuses to market.

**Sentence length:** average 6–12 words; max ~18. Fragments are normal. Lists over paragraphs. Metadata displayed as content.

**Verb energy:** imperative and flat. No adverbs. No softening. "Buy." "Read." "Don't." The writing matches the 2px borders — nothing rounded.

**Vocabulary:** make, press, ship, buy, read, write, print, mail, stop. Plain. Numbers and units live in the copy ("300 pressed. 214 left. $26."). Avoid: anything that sounds like a brand agency wrote it. Avoid adjectives ending in -ing (amazing, stunning, exciting). Avoid marketing verbs (leverage, empower, unlock, drive).

**Punctuation:** hard periods. Double slashes // for metadata. ALL CAPS for emphasis, used as a statement not decoration. No em-dashes (use hyphens or periods). No exclamation marks unless the whole design is shouting, and then only once.

**Headline pattern:**
- "A record pressed by hand, mailed by humans, priced for listeners."
- "300 COPIES. ONCE A MONTH. NO REPRESSES."
- "BUY IT. OR DON'T."

**Body pattern:**
> {Brand} releases one vinyl and one cassette every month. 300 copies each. Numbered. Signed by the artist if you ask. No digital-only exclusives. No NFTs. No algorithm.

> PE-142 // 12" LP // 300 pressed / 214 left. Mira Halász. Ships 2026-05-02. $26. First-class mail. No tracking under $40.

**CTA language:** "Buy.", "Listen.", "Subscribe.", "Read.", "Pre-order →". One word plus a period. The arrow is optional and deliberate.

**Never says:**
- "Elevate your listening experience"
- "Curated for discerning collectors"
- Anything with an em-dash dramatic pause
- "Join our community"
- Emoji

## 7. Vibrant-Playful

**One-line:** warm, confident, human — friendly without being cute, alive without being loud.

**Sentence length:** average 10–16 words; max ~22. Mix of rhythms. One punchy sentence per paragraph earns its keep.

**Verb energy:** active and specific. Uses "you" naturally. Imperatives are energetic but not shouty ("Try the canvas," not "Unlock your creativity now!"). Verbs do the work; adjectives stay home.

**Vocabulary:** sketch, ship, share, draft, comment, export, together, twelve (specific numbers over vague ones). Avoid: magical, effortless, delightful (as a puff word), "supercharge," "unleash," "revolutionize," "game-changer." Warm does not mean cutesy.

**Punctuation:** periods and commas, used briskly. Em-dashes for pacing. One exclamation mark per page, maximum, and only if it's load-bearing. Ampersands allowed as a casual marker.

**Headline pattern:**
- "Sketch, comment, ship — without the Slack ping."
- "Free to start. Scale when you're ready."
- "Twelve designers, one canvas, no export hell."

**Body pattern:**
> Twelve designers can live-edit the same board. Every stroke has history, every comment has a cursor, and every export has a handoff link that doesn't go stale.

> For one-designer shops, side projects, and curious creators. Everything in Solo is free, forever. Upgrade when you outgrow it — not before.

**CTA language:** "Try the canvas," "See how it works," "Start free," "Get the app," "Join the beta." Verb-forward, friendly, never more than four words. No "Unlock" or "Supercharge."

**Never says:**
- "Supercharge your workflow"
- "Unleash your creativity"
- "Magical experience"
- "AI-powered" (if that's the product, say what it actually does)
- "The future of ___"

## Cross-direction anti-patterns: forbidden regardless of direction

These phrases are AI tells. They make the copy look generated even when the visuals are right. Filter them out on the last pass, every time, every direction.

**Verbs to kill:** unlock, empower, leverage, harness, supercharge, unleash, revolutionize, disrupt, transform (as a hype verb), streamline, optimize (as a puff word), elevate.

**Adjectives to kill:** seamless, seamlessly, powerful, robust, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, next-generation, best-in-class, world-class, industry-leading, game-changing, revolutionary, innovative (as a self-description), magical, effortless, intuitive (tell it, don't say it).

**Phrases to kill:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Harness the power of..."
- "Unlock your potential"
- "Take your ___ to the next level"
- "The future of ___"
- "Join thousands of happy customers"
- "Trusted by leading brands"
- "One-stop shop"
- "End-to-end solution"
- "At the intersection of ___ and ___"
- "We're on a mission to..."

**Openers to refuse:** "Introducing..." (unless it really is an announcement and even then consider rewriting), "Imagine a world where...", "What if...", "In a time when..."

**Structural tells:** three-bullet feature lists that all start with the same verb, question-answer pairs pretending to be FAQ, "But wait, there's more" escalations, everything-is-a-promise writing ("We believe ___").

## Length targets by slot

| Direction | Hero headline | Subline | CTA | Body para |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Editorial | ≤ 12 words | 30–60 words | ≤ 4 words | 50–120 words |
| Technical-Minimal | ≤ 10 words | 20–35 words | ≤ 3 words | 20–50 words |
| Bold-Distinctive | ≤ 7 words | 15–25 words | ≤ 3 words | 25–60 words |
| Dark-Premium | ≤ 9 words | 20–40 words | ≤ 4 words | 30–70 words |
| Warm-Approachable | ≤ 10 words | 20–35 words | ≤ 4 words | 30–60 words |
| Brutalist | ≤ 8 words | 12–25 words | 1 word + "." | 15–40 words |
| Vibrant-Playful | ≤ 9 words | 20–35 words | ≤ 4 words | 25–50 words |

Exceed these only with reason. "This sounds better at 15 words" is not a reason; "this is an editorial long-form intro paragraph and the direction expects it" is.

## How to apply

1. Before writing any copy, open this file and find the direction profile that matches the one in `design-system.md` (or the active starter in `assets/direction-starters/`).
2. Draft the copy aiming at the sentence-length target and verb energy for that direction.
3. Run the **cross-direction forbidden-word filter** as a last pass. If any banned verb, adjective, or phrase is in the draft, rewrite the sentence — not just the word. (A sentence built around "empower" stays bad after you swap in "enable.")
4. Read the draft aloud. Does it sound like the visual it's sitting on? If the visual is a quiet Editorial page and the copy crackles like a startup launch, the copy loses. Rewrite.
5. If the user has requested a direction but the copy they provided fights it (a Brutalist zine with "empower your listening experience"), flag the mismatch and propose revised copy in-voice. Do not silently translate — show the delta.

## Worked examples

Three structural slots — landing hero, pricing tier description, error message — rendered in three different direction voices. Same job, different register. Read across the row.

### Landing hero (headline + subline + CTAs)

**Technical-Minimal**
> **Every dollar posted, categorized, and reconciled the moment it moves.**
> No import jobs. No reconciliation queue. No month-end archaeology. The ledger is the product — not a report you run once a month.
> [Open account →] [Read the docs]

**Editorial**
> **A quieter case for finance done well.**
> Somewhere between the spreadsheet and the quarterly review, a habit hardened: treat the ledger as a chore and the close as a crisis. {Brand} argues for the opposite — that the books should be correct as they happen, and that trust in the numbers is the first product of a finance function worth keeping.
> [Read the issue]  [Subscribe]

**Brutalist**
> **LEDGER. LIVE. NO CLOSE.**
> Every transaction posts when it happens. Not at month-end. Not overnight. When it happens. 4,200 companies on it. $38B posted last quarter. Read the source: /docs.
> [Sign up.]  [Read the docs.]

### Pricing tier description (one tier, featured)

**Vibrant-Playful**
> **Team — $29/mo per editor**
> For design teams of 3 to 20. Unlimited boards, version history, and a handoff link that doesn't go stale. Everything in Solo, plus live cursors and comment threads.
> [Start free trial]

**Dark-Premium**
> **Team. $29 per editor, per month.**
> For design teams of three to twenty. Unlimited boards. Version history retained indefinitely. Handoff links that remain valid through the life of the project. Live collaboration and threaded review included.
> [Reserve a seat]

**Warm-Approachable**
> **Team plan — $29 per person, each month**
> For small teams — roughly three to twenty of you. You get unlimited boards, full version history, and handoff links that won't break on your clients. Live cursors, comments, all the good stuff. Cancel whenever; we won't make it awkward.
> [Start your free trial]

### Error message (500, database unreachable)

**Technical-Minimal**
> **500 — database unreachable**
> Request ID: req_a7f3e9. We've been paged. Try again in a minute or check status.{brand}.com.
> [Retry]  [Status page]

**Brutalist**
> **500.**
> DATABASE UNREACHABLE. req_a7f3e9 // 2026-04-20 14:22 UTC. Nothing you did. Try again.
> [RETRY.]

**Warm-Approachable**
> **Something's off on our end.**
> Sorry — the database isn't picking up right now. We've already been notified and someone's on it. If you try again in a minute, it'll probably just work. If it doesn't, reply to this with request ID req_a7f3e9 and we'll dig in.
> [Try again]

---

The test, after every draft: would the copy fit on the page if you changed nothing but the CSS direction? If yes, it's too generic. Every direction should be able to claim its copy and none of the others should want it.
