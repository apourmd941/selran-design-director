Essay Issue IV by Emma Fallon

On the virtue of slow software.

A reconsideration of the tools we reach for in the morning, and what happens when an app earns a second minute of our attention rather than a second of it.

Somewhere between the notifications and the tabs, a habit hardened. We opened the app, glanced at it, closed it, opened it again — an endless loop of near-attention that accomplished nothing but filled the day. The app was designed for it. Every surface was a summary; every summary gestured toward another.

There is a quieter argument to be made. Software can ask for less of us and give more back. A single view, held steady, that rewards the act of looking. A reading list that does not refresh on pull. A page that loads once and stays loaded. None of this is impossible; most of it is unfashionable.

The question is not whether we have time for slow software. The question is what the fast software has been spending our time on. A morning spent glancing is a morning spent glancing. A morning spent reading is, usually, a better morning.
